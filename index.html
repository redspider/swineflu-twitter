What is swine influenza?

Swine influenza is the common name now given to a new type of flu virus, derived in part from human influenza virus type A subtype H1N1, and in part from strains of the influenza virus usually found in pigs.

The new virus appears to transmit from human to human, and indications so far suggest it is capable of becoming a pandemic.

Prevention

Preventing infection

Avoid close contact
Avoid close contact with people who are sick. When you are sick, keep your distance from others to help protect them.
Stay home when sick
If at all possible, stay home from work, school and errands when you are sick to help prevent others from catching your illness.
Cover your mouth and nose
When coughing or sneezing, cover your mouth and nose with a tissue. Dispose of tissues promptly and carefully.
Clean your hands
Wash your hands often with soap and water. Alcohol-based hand sanitisers are also effective.
Avoid touching your eyes, nose or mouth
The virus is often spread when a person touches something that is contaminated, then touches his or her eyes, nose or mouth.
Practice other good health habits
Get plenty of sleep, be physically active, manage stress, drink plenty of fluids and eat nutritious food.

Prevention questions

Can eating pork result in infection?
No. Eating properly handled and cooked pork and pork products is safe. Cooking pork to an internal temperature of at least 72°C (160°F) kills the swine flu virus as it does other pathogens.
Will Tamiflu or Relenza help?
Yes. Tamiflu and Relenza, both antivirals, appear to be effective against this strain at this time.
Will antibiotics help?
No. Antibiotics are only effective against bacteria, not viruses. However, your doctor may prescribe antibiotics to deal with secondary infections during treatment.
Will natural remedies help?
No. There are no known natural remedies capable of fighting a virus directly. Secondary effects such as improving immune response are unknown.
I was vaccinated against the normal flu, will this help?
No. Definitive information is not yet available, but the consensus seems to be that the vaccinations given for the current seasonal flu will not prevent infection by swine influenza.
Will wearing a mask help?
Yes. Masks can help reduce the risk of infection, both directly and by preventing the wearer from touching the nose or mouth. They must be used correctly however, and are only a limited solution. Masks should be available from your pharmacist.

Symptoms and treatment

How do I know if I've got it?

Swine influenza can cause a wide range of symptoms, including fever, cough, sore throat, body aches, headache, chills and fatigue. Some people have reported diarrhea and vomiting associated with swine flu. As with regular flu, swine influenza can vary from mild to severe: pneumonia, respiratory failure and even death is possible.

It takes between 1 and 3 days for the infection to become apparent.

When to seek medical attention

If you have flu symptoms, telephone your doctor, your normal medical professional for advice, or call Healthline on 0800 611 116. To avoid infecting others, don't visit your doctor until you have spoken with a medical professional.

However, if you have any of the following symptoms, you should seek emergency medical care immediately:

  • Difficulty breathing or chest pain
  • Purple or blue discolouration of the lips
  • Vomiting and unable to keep liquids down
  • Signs of dehydration such as dizziness when standing, absence of urination, or in infants, a lack of tears when they cry
  • Less responsive than normal or becomes confused
In children, the following additional warning signs apply:
  • Fast breathing or trouble breathing
  • Bluish skin color
  • Not drinking enough fluids
  • Not waking up or not interacting
  • Being so irritable that the child does not want to be held
  • Flu-like symptoms improve but then return with a fever and worse cough
  • Fever with a rash
If in doubt, seek care. Better safe than sorry.

Important home treatment notes

The CDC has a comprehensive home care guide
Please see Interim Guidance for Swine Influenza: Taking Care of a Sick Person in Your Home
Do not use medicines containing aspirin for children or teenagers
Use of aspirin to treat symptoms of viral illnesses increases the risk of developing Reye's syndrome. Ibuprofen (Nurofen) and similar are OK.
Stay at home for 7 days after symptoms cease
In order to avoid exposing others to the virus, you should remain at home for 7 days after your symptoms have ceased. For children, this should be extended to 10 days.
